# 03 — Epistemic-Network Simulation Literature

**Exec summary (3 sentences).** The epistemic-network literature has a robust subgenre — the Zollman / Bala-Goyal / O'Connor lineage — in which fully Bayesian agents on a graph demonstrably converge onto the *inferior* of two bandit arms because early bad luck plus social pooling cuts off exploration of the better arm; this gives a discrete-action, multi-attractor analogue of "paradigm lock-in" without requiring any non-Bayesian heuristic. The mechanism is *absorbing-state* rather than *bistability of a continuous dynamical system*: agents stop pulling the un-favoured arm, so no fresh evidence updates the posterior, freezing the population at a sub-optimal consensus. Grafting this onto our v2 substrate requires (i) replacing continuous θ with at least two discrete hypotheses/actions, and (ii) making sampling endogenous (an agent who currently believes paradigm A only generates data informative about A) — the trust matrix and Gamma-precision machinery survive almost unchanged.

Key load-bearing question for our paper: **Zollman-style lock-in DOES survive under Bayesian update** — it is generated by Bayesian conditionalization on a self-censoring data stream, not by a heuristic. The non-robustness shown by Rosenstock-Bruner-O'Connor (2017) is about *which network topology helps most*, not about whether lock-in exists.

## 1. Zollman 2007 — "The Communication Structure of Epistemic Communities"

```bibtex
@article{zollman2007communication,
  author  = {Zollman, Kevin J. S.},
  title   = {The Communication Structure of Epistemic Communities},
  journal = {Philosophy of Science},
  volume  = {74},
  number  = {5},
  pages   = {574--587},
  year    = {2007},
  doi     = {10.1086/525605}
}
```

- **Code:** Reimplementation in Python at <https://github.com/jweisber/sep-sen> (`zollman.py`); NetLogo replication at <https://github.com/daimpi/SocNetABM>.
- **Mechanism (1 sentence):** Bayesian agents on a graph pick one of two binomial bandits per round, share evidence with neighbours, and converge — denser networks more often lock onto the *inferior* arm because a run of unlucky pulls on the true-better arm is broadcast widely and convinces the whole population to stop sampling it, after which no further evidence ever arrives.
- **Graft onto our `src/`:** Replace continuous μ in `inference.py` with a discrete posterior over K hypotheses (Dirichlet-categorical or just two Beta heads). Add an *action gate* in `policy.py`: an agent only emits an observation for hypothesis k if its current MAP/best-arm choice is k. Keep `trust.py` as the pooling kernel — Zollman uses uniform averaging, but row-stochastic trust generalises it. `world.py` becomes a K-armed Bernoulli generator. This is the most direct path to a multi-attractor substrate.

## 2. Zollman 2010 — "The Epistemic Benefit of Transient Diversity"

```bibtex
@article{zollman2010transient,
  author  = {Zollman, Kevin J. S.},
  title   = {The Epistemic Benefit of Transient Diversity},
  journal = {Erkenntnis},
  volume  = {72},
  number  = {1},
  pages   = {17--35},
  year    = {2010},
  doi     = {10.1007/s10670-009-9194-6}
}
```

- **Code:** Same Weisberg repo (<https://github.com/jweisber/sep-sen>); `daimpi/SocNetABM` is explicitly built on the 2010 model.
- **Mechanism (1 sentence):** With **Beta-Bernoulli Bayesian agents**, division of labour is sustained either by limiting communication *or* by extreme priors; when both are present, the population settles into permanent disagreement and never converges — the substrate has **multiple absorbing states** even though every agent is a textbook conjugate Bayesian.
- **Graft:** Same as above plus an *extreme-prior* knob. Map this to our `inference.py` initial μ₀ / κ₀ heterogeneity (which v2 already supports) but combined with action-gating so heterogeneous priors persist instead of washing out (cf. our v2 finding that μ₀ / τ₀ heterogeneity washes out fast). The "extreme priors" knob is the same shape as our existing `kappa0` heterogeneity, but it only bites once sampling is endogenous.

## 3. Bala & Goyal 1998 — "Learning from Neighbours"

```bibtex
@article{bala1998learning,
  author  = {Bala, Venkatesh and Goyal, Sanjeev},
  title   = {Learning from Neighbours},
  journal = {The Review of Economic Studies},
  volume  = {65},
  number  = {3},
  pages   = {595--621},
  year    = {1998},
  doi     = {10.1111/1467-937X.00059}
}
```

- **Code:** No canonical public repo found — it's an analytical paper, but the model is reimplemented inside `sep-sen` and inside O'Connor & Weatherall's downstream work.
- **Mechanism (1 sentence):** In a connected society of Bayesian agents who choose actions based on own + neighbours' experience, the population reaches **uniform conformism** in the long run, but the conformist action need not be the optimal one — exactly because once everyone abandons action B no further data on B is ever produced (the "no-experimentation absorbing trap").
- **Graft:** This is the analytical justification of the absorbing-state mechanism; cite as the canonical *theorem* underwriting the Zollman simulations. No code change beyond §1 needed.

## 4. Rosenstock, Bruner & O'Connor 2017 — "In Epistemic Networks, Is Less Really More?"

```bibtex
@article{rosenstock2017epistemic,
  author  = {Rosenstock, Sarita and Bruner, Justin and O'Connor, Cailin},
  title   = {In Epistemic Networks, Is Less Really More?},
  journal = {Philosophy of Science},
  volume  = {84},
  number  = {2},
  pages   = {234--252},
  year    = {2017},
  doi     = {10.1086/690717}
}
```

- **Code:** Replicated in `jweisber/sep-sen` (`rbo-*.py`).
- **Mechanism (1 sentence):** The Zollman *network advantage* (sparser = better) is **not robust** to parameter choices (number of pulls, arm-gap ε, population size) — but lock-in onto the inferior arm itself remains a generic outcome; the paper *tightens* the claim rather than refuting it.
- **Graft:** Important *honesty* citation — if we adopt a Zollman-style mechanism, our claims must be parameter-robust. Forces us to do a sweep over (ε, n, network density) just as RBO did. No `src/` change, but a notebook contract: any sweep must include a robustness panel.

```bibtex
@book{oconnor2019misinformation,

  title     = {The Misinformation Age: How False Beliefs Spread},
  publisher = {Yale University Press},
  year      = {2019}
}
@article{weatherall2020conformity,
  author  = {Weatherall, James Owen and O'Connor, Cailin},
  title   = {Conformity in Scientific Networks},
  journal = {Synthese},
  volume  = {198},
  number  = {8},
  pages   = {7257--7278},
  year    = {2021},
  doi     = {10.1007/s11229-019-02520-2}
}
```

- **Code:** `ow.py`, `ow-animate.py` in <https://github.com/jweisber/sep-sen>.
- **Mechanism (1 sentence):** Built directly on Bala-Goyal: Bayesian agents on a graph with optional **mistrust weights** (agents discount neighbours whose past beliefs differed from theirs) generate **stable polarised clusters** because mistrust hardens into permanent informational segregation — this maps almost exactly onto our trust matrix `T`.
- **Graft:** Best fit for our **existing trust machinery**. Their mistrust update rule is a Beta-likelihood on "did neighbour's past report match my world-model?". Replace our row-stochastic trust pooling kernel with their endogenous mistrust update in `trust.py` and we get polarised attractors out of the box, *without* abandoning Bayesian agents.

- **Graft:** Drop-in for our substrate: set one agent's `kappa0` or precision-prior to infinity in `inference.py` (no learning). Pairs naturally with the "vanguard" notebook 10 finding (isolated-vanguard overconfidence flooding the mainstream). One-line change; high explanatory payoff.

## 7. Mohseni & Williams 2021 — "Truth and Conformity on Networks"

```bibtex
@article{mohseni2021truth,
  author  = {Mohseni, Aydin and Williams, Cole Randall},
  title   = {Truth and Conformity on Networks},
  journal = {Erkenntnis},
  volume  = {86},
  number  = {6},
  pages   = {1509--1530},
  year    = {2021},
  doi     = {10.1007/s10670-019-00167-6}
}
```

- **Code:** Mohseni publishes some sims at <http://aydinmohseni.com/research/> but no consolidated repo for this paper was located — flag as "no public code confirmed".
- **Mechanism (1 sentence):** Bayesian agents who additionally pay a *conformity cost* for publicly disagreeing with neighbours falsify their reports; downstream Bayesians condition on the falsified reports and the network locks onto the conformist consensus regardless of private evidence — a **public-vs-private belief gap** that breaks ground-truth convergence.
- **Graft:** Add a "report" layer on top of our existing pooling: each agent emits a *biased* μ to neighbours = argmin over the trust-weighted disagreement cost. Lives in `policy.py` (action selection) and `trust.py` (consumers). Heavier modification, but it is the only mechanism in this list that does not require discrete arms.

```bibtex
  number  = {2},
  pages   = {454--464},
  year    = {2024},
  doi     = {10.1177/17456916231180100}
}
```

- **Code:** No paper-specific repo found; Smaldino's other ABMs are on <https://github.com/smaldino>.
- **Mechanism (1 sentence):** Survey/synthesis: across psychology, organisation science and philosophy ABMs, premature consensus on inferior solutions is the *default* failure mode; transient diversity is restored by **behavioural inertia, transmission noise, or sparse networks** — any one is enough.

## Cross-cutting summary

| Paper | Bayesian agents? | Mechanism class | Public code | Graft size |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Zollman 2007 | yes (Beta-Bernoulli) | bandit + action-gating | yes (Weisberg, NetLogo) | discrete-arm rewrite of `world.py` |
| Zollman 2010 | yes | bandit + extreme priors | yes | same + heterogeneity knob |
| Bala-Goyal 98 | yes | bandit (analytical) | no | (theory only) |
| RBO 2017 | yes | robustness critique | yes (Weisberg) | sweep protocol |
| O'Connor-Weatherall | yes | **endogenous mistrust trust matrix** | yes (Weisberg) | drop-in for our `trust.py` |
| Holman-Bruner | yes + 1 stubborn | fixed-prior outlier | no canonical | 1-line in `inference.py` |
| Mohseni-Williams | yes + conformity cost | reporting layer | not located | adds report layer to `policy.py` |
| Smaldino 2024 | survey | inertia / noise / sparsity | no | umbrella citation |
| Golub-Jackson | naive DeGroot | linear consensus (contrast) | no | framing of negative result |
| Frey-Šešelja | yes + inertia | switching threshold | yes (ArgABM) | δ-threshold in `policy.py` |

**Bottom-line answer to the load-bearing question:** Zollman-style lock-in is *generated by* Bayesian update, not despite it — the absorbing-state mechanism needs (a) discrete actions, (b) endogenous sampling, and (c) social pooling, all of which are compatible with Gaussian/Beta-conjugate updates. We can keep our agents Bayesian.
