# Literature Review: Cultural Evolution and Prestige — Big Men, CREDs, Attentional Inheritance

**Cluster:** C (cultural-evolution-prestige)
**Date:** 2026-05-04
**Scope:** Prestige, CREDs, big-man leadership, leveling, attentional inheritance — the cultural-evolution mechanisms by which values are transmitted via community attention allocation.

---

## Key Findings

### 1. Prestige is operationally an attention asymmetry, not a status label
The foundational Henrich & Gil-White (2001) move was to define prestige as *freely conferred deference* rather than as fear-based dominance. The mechanistic content of "deference" turns out to be largely *attentional*: copiers monitor whom *other* learners are watching, listening to, sitting near, gifting, and asking. Chudek, Heller, Birch & Henrich (2012) made this experimentally concrete in 3–4-year-olds: ten seconds of differential bystander gaze toward one of two demonstrators more than doubled the odds children later copied that demonstrator on a wholly unrelated task. The prestige cue *is* the redistribution of community attention; the copier infers "this person is worth attending to" because the community already does. For a salience-as-prior thesis this is the clearest empirical demonstration that the population's attention pattern functions as a distributed second-order prior over whom is worth attending to — a prior that learners read off the social field and internalize.

### 2. The "big man" form is a leveling-compatible attention concentrator
Sahlins's (1963) ideal-typical contrast — Melanesian big-man (status earned anew each generation through generosity, oratory, feast-throwing) versus Polynesian chief (ascribed hereditary office) — describes two regimes for *concentrating* community attention. Crucially, Boehm (1999, 2012) shows that small-scale egalitarian societies actively *prevent* attention from coagulating into hereditary rank using counter-dominance coalitions, mockery, gossip, ostracism, and (in extremis) execution — all of which are mechanisms for *withdrawing* community attention from would-be dominants. Henrich, Chudek & Boyd (2015) — "The Big Man Mechanism" — formalizes how prestige (not dominance) can solve collective action problems precisely *because* it leaves leaders with no coercive lever: their only currency is the attention/copying their behavior commands, which in turn requires they remain prosocial and generous. Prestige and egalitarianism are thus not opposites but co-constituting: prestige is the attention regime that survives leveling. This is exactly what the salience-prior view predicts — values are made operative as the shape of which behaviors community attention amplifies and which it withdraws from.

### 3. CREDs are a costly-signal solution to verbal cheap-talk about values
Henrich (2009) argues that as language let humans transmit beliefs cheaply, learners faced an inference problem: which professed beliefs do models *actually* hold (and so are worth copying)? Credibility-Enhancing Displays — fasting, celibacy, painful initiations, martyrdom, public sacrifice — are actions only worth performing if the agent really holds the belief. Lanman & Buhrmester (2017) operationalized this with a CRED-exposure scale and showed childhood exposure to parental CREDs predicts adult theism better than analytic-thinking or existential-insecurity accounts; Turpin et al. (2019, *Evol. Human Sciences*) showed declining CREDs predict secularization. Reframed as priors: a CRED is what raises the precision (confidence) of the prior the learner adopts. Without CREDs, models' verbal claims are low-precision evidence; with CREDs, they are high-precision and therefore reshape the learner's prior structure. This is a near-direct mapping from Henrich's framework into precision-weighting language.

### 4. Even the *attentional infrastructure* is culturally inherited (Heyes)
Heyes's (2018) "cognitive gadgets" thesis pushes harder than standard dual-inheritance: the very mechanisms of selective social learning — including which faces, voices, and behaviors children attend to — are themselves culturally constructed during development. Her 2024 *Perspectives on Psychological Science* "Rethinking Norm Psychology" extends this to norms: normative competence is a culturally assembled cognitive gadget on top of a domain-general genetic "starter kit" of attentional and motivational biases. The decisive observation: "there is evidence that contemporary humans can socially learn attentional biases, specifically by learning to attend more to some agents than others by following the gaze of third parties." This is the direct cultural inheritance of the salience landscape itself. For our thesis it is load-bearing: it answers the obvious objection "but surely attention is hardwired" — no, the *contents* of attention (whom to watch, what to find exemplary) are themselves transmitted culturally.

### 5. The TTOM/cultural-affordances bridge already maps prestige to active inference (partly)
Veissière, Constant, Ramstead, Friston & Kirmayer (2020, *BBS*) — "Thinking Through Other Minds" — and Ramstead, Veissière & Kirmayer (2016, *Frontiers in Psychology*) — "Cultural Affordances: Scaffolding Local Worlds Through Shared Intentionality and Regimes of Attention" — explicitly cast cultural learning as variational inference where "regimes of attention" mark certain actions as salient, generating action policies that minimize free energy. They cite Henrich-tradition work and use phrases like "regimes of shared attention" that are essentially co-extensive with what cultural-evolution theorists call prestige-biased social learning. The bridge is partly built; what's missing in their account is an explicit treatment of *prestige cues* and *CREDs* as the population-level mechanism that calibrates the precision of which model's expectations to internalize. That gap is exactly the contribution our paper can make.

## Must-Read Shortlist

### The Evolution of Prestige: Freely Conferred Deference as a Mechanism for Enhancing the Benefits of Cultural Transmission — Henrich & Gil-White (2001)
- **Venue:** Evolution and Human Behavior 22(3), 165–196
- **DOI:** 10.1016/S1090-5138(00)00071-4
- **Central claim:** Humans evolved a psychology that uses indirect cues — especially others' deference, gaze, proximity, and copying — to identify high-quality cultural models worth learning from. Prestige is structurally distinct from primate dominance: it is freely conferred *because* deference buys access to information, and access is gated by attention.
- **Why it matters for our thesis:** This is the canonical statement that whom-to-copy is solved by reading the *community's* attention distribution rather than by direct skill assessment. The salience landscape is the prior. Every later prestige paper traces back here.
- **Formal apparatus to borrow:** Conceptual rather than formal in this paper; the formal models came later (see Henrich & Boyd 1998 conformist coevolution, Egozi & Ram 2024 below).

### Prestige-biased cultural learning: bystander's differential attention to potential models influences children's learning — Chudek, Heller, Birch & Henrich (2012)
- **Venue:** Evolution and Human Behavior 33(1), 46–56
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.evolhumbehav.2011.05.005
- **Central claim:** A purely attentional cue (10 s of bystander gaze toward one of two adults) more than doubles 3–4-year-olds' likelihood of copying that adult on novel tasks, including in different domains than the one in which prestige was established.
- **Why it matters for our thesis:** This is the cleanest experimental demonstration that *attention by others* is itself the prestige cue children read. The prior is read off the field of community attention. Domain-generality is also crucial: prestige is not skill-specific but globalizes — i.e., it functions as a *general* high-precision flag.
- **Formal apparatus to borrow:** Empirical, not formal; the design is reusable as a paradigm for active-inference experiments on social precision.

### The Big Man Mechanism: how prestige fosters cooperation and creates prosocial leaders — Henrich, Chudek & Boyd (2015)
- **Venue:** Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B 370(1683), 20150013
- **DOI:** 10.1098/rstb.2015.0013
- **Central claim:** A culture–gene coevolutionary model showing prestige-based leadership generates correlated phenotypes between leaders and followers (because followers copy), which in turn selects (genetically) for leaders who are more prosocial — *but only when groups are small*. In larger groups the mechanism shifts to cultural transmission rather than direct deference.
- **Why it matters for our thesis:** Demonstrates the loop. Community attention concentrates on a "big man"; followers copy his behavior (including prosocial dispositions); this selects for prosocial leadership; norms stabilize. Prestige is the attentional channel through which the value loop closes.
- **Formal apparatus to borrow:** Yes — explicit population-genetic / cultural-transmission model with leader-follower correlation. Worth reading for the math on how attention-mediated copying produces phenotype correlation.

### The Evolution of Costly Displays, Cooperation and Religion: CREDs and their Implications for Cultural Evolution — Henrich (2009)
- **Venue:** Evolution and Human Behavior 30(4), 244–260
- **DOI:** 10.1016/j.evolhumbehav.2009.03.005
- **Central claim:** Once symbolic communication enabled cheap transmission of beliefs, natural selection favored learners who weighted models' verbal claims by costly behavioral displays of commitment to those claims (CREDs). CREDs interlock with beliefs to produce stable culturally-evolved equilibria where costly practices persist.
- **Why it matters for our thesis:** Direct precision-weighting analog. CREDs raise the *precision* of prior preferences over actions that the learner adopts from the model. Without CREDs, professed values are low-precision; with CREDs, they reshape the prior. This is the cleanest place where Henrich's framework asks to be reformulated in active-inference language.
- **Formal apparatus to borrow:** Game-theoretic / signaling model in the appendix, plus a cultural-evolutionary stability analysis. The signaling formalism translates straightforwardly to a precision-modulation story.

### Cognitive Gadgets: The Cultural Evolution of Thinking — Heyes (2018) [book] + Rethinking Norm Psychology — Heyes (2024)
- **Venue:** Harvard University Press (book) / Perspectives on Psychological Science 19(1) (2024 paper)
- **DOI:** 10.4159/9780674985155 (book) / 10.1177/17456916221112075 (2024 paper)
- **Central claim:** The mechanisms of selective social learning — including the attentional biases that determine *whom* to copy — are themselves culturally inherited cognitive gadgets, not fixed genetic adaptations. Genetic evolution provides only a domain-general "starter kit" of social attentional biases (faces, voices, social rewards). Norms are then assembled on top via cultural learning.
- **Why it matters for our thesis:** Crucial for the strong reading of our claim. If the salience landscape *is* the values, then it must itself be inheritable — and Heyes shows it is. Children learn what to attend to by following the gaze of third parties; this is attentional inheritance in the sense the paper requires.
- **Formal apparatus to borrow:** Conceptual; the 2024 paper in particular gives a clean implicit/explicit + genetic-starter / cultural-gadget typology that can be re-mapped into a hierarchy of prior precisions.

### Thinking Through Other Minds: A Variational Approach to Cognition and Culture — Veissière, Constant, Ramstead, Friston & Kirmayer (2020) [BRIDGE PAPER]
- **Venue:** Behavioral and Brain Sciences 43, e90
- **DOI:** 10.1017/S0140525X19001213
- **Central claim:** Cultural learning is variational inference: humans build social niches stocked with "cultural affordances" via "regimes of attention" that mark certain actions as salient. Learning a culture *is* learning to predict what others will predict.
- **Why it matters for our thesis:** This is the most direct existing bridge between active inference and the Henrich-tradition cultural-evolution literature. The "regimes of attention" construct is essentially what we're calling a salience prior. The paper does *not* fully integrate prestige and CREDs as the population-level calibration mechanism — that gap is our contribution.
- **Formal apparatus to borrow:** Free-energy formalism applied to cultural learning, including precision-weighting of social information. See also Ramstead, Veissière & Kirmayer (2016, *Frontiers in Psychology*, doi:10.3389/fpsyg.2016.01090) for the underlying cultural-affordances framework.

### Prestige bias in cultural evolutionary dynamics — Egozi & Ram (2024)
- **Venue:** Royal Society Open Science 11(7), 230650
- **DOI:** 10.1098/rsos.230650
- **Central claim:** Mathematical analysis + stochastic simulations of prestige-biased transmission. Key result: success bias plays the role of natural selection, while prestige bias plays the role of genetic drift — accelerating dynamics through a "rich-get-richer" rather than fitness-tracking mechanism. Prestige can therefore propagate maladaptive variants.
- **Why it matters for our thesis:** Most up-to-date formal model of prestige dynamics; gives us a population-level dynamical system to plug precision-weighted attention into. Also useful for the failure mode argument (when shared priors lock in maladaptive norms because attention concentrates on a high-prestige model regardless of ground truth).
- **Formal apparatus to borrow:** Stochastic role-model choice with analytic approximations for fixation probability/time. This is the right substrate to extend with precision-modulated priors.

## Surprising / Counterintuitive Findings

1. **Prestige bias generalizes across domains in 4-year-olds.** Chudek et al. (2012) found that an attentional cue established in one domain (e.g., artifact-use) transferred to copying in a totally unrelated domain (food/drink preferences). Prestige is not a domain-specific "this person knows tools" tag; it functions as a *global* high-precision flag that says "everything this person does is worth attending to." This is exactly the behavior of a precision parameter rather than a content prior.

2. **Egalitarian societies are not flat — they actively suppress hierarchy by withdrawing attention.** Boehm's reverse-dominance hypothesis frames egalitarianism as effortful work: gossip, mockery, and ostracism are the *positive* mechanisms, and they work by stripping the would-be dominant of community attention. So-called "flat" societies are actually densely attention-managed. This recasts norms as the equilibrium of a community-wide attention-allocation game rather than as rules everyone individually follows.

3. **In the Egozi & Ram (2024) formal model, prestige bias acts like genetic drift, not selection.** Counterintuitive because prestige is intuitively about "copy the best." Mathematically, copying-the-already-copied amplifies whatever happens to be currently attended, regardless of fitness — so prestige can lock in maladaptive norms through pure rich-get-richer dynamics. Failure mode: shared high-precision priors don't have to be *good* priors. This is a useful place where the salience-prior framework predicts failure modes (echo chambers, fashion cascades, status-driven malinvestment) the cultural-evolution literature already documents.

4. **Bridge papers exist but are incomplete.** Veissière et al. (2020) and Ramstead et al. (2016) explicitly link active inference to cultural transmission via "regimes of attention," but neither integrates the prestige/CREDs mechanism as the population-level calibration of which models' priors propagate. This is a real gap — and a thesis-shaped one.

## Open Questions / Gaps

- No paper (to our knowledge) explicitly models CREDs as precision-modulators in an active-inference framework. Henrich (2009) gives the signaling logic; Friston-tradition work gives the precision formalism; nobody has joined them.
- The attention-withdrawal mechanism (Boehm) lacks a formal active-inference treatment. Withdrawing attention should reduce a model's effective precision in the population's collective inference; this is unmodeled.
- Heyes's "attentional inheritance" claim (we learn whom to attend to by tracking third-party gaze) is empirically supported in development but has not been integrated with precision-weighting. There's a clean paper to write here.
- The Egozi & Ram (2024) drift-not-selection result about prestige is a perfect entry point for the failure-mode argument, but the original framework doesn't connect to active inference.
- Singh & Glowacki's (2022) "beyond nomadic-egalitarian" model suggests the Pleistocene ancestral environment was more variable than the standard egalitarian story; this complicates simple narratives about which attention regime is "ancestral."
