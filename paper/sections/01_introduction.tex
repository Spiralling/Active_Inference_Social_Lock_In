% =====================================================================
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% =====================================================================

Norms --- shared regularities of behaviour through which agents with
heterogeneous preferences coordinate --- are studied separately across
game theory \citep{koster2022SillyRules,oldenburgZhiXuan2024BayesianRule},
cultural evolution
\citep{henrich2009CREDs,henrichGilWhite2001Prestige,heyes2018CognitiveGadgets},
and active inference
\citep{constant2019Regimes,albarracin2022EpistemicCommunities}, with
limited integration. The substrate problem common to all three is how
the priors that make a norm binding for an individual agent come to be
shared, maintained, and revised across a community.

This paper proposes a single technical apparatus for that problem.
Norms are formalised as \emph{salience priors}: high-precision
components of an agent's prior preference distribution
$\C$ that direct attention to particular features of the environment.
The community-level dynamics of these priors are governed by two
precision-related quantities. First, the precision $\trust{i}{j}$ that
agent $i$ assigns to the channel from neighbouring agent $j$ is
\emph{learned}, in the same Bayesian sense as any other channel
precision in active inference: it tracks the prediction error that
$j$'s prior actions induced about subsequently observed environmental
ground truth. Trust, on this view, is not a separate module; it is
what precision-weighting looks like when one of the agent's evidence
channels happens to be another agent. Second, the agent's own update
to $\C$ is gated by a \emph{variational cost of changing one's mind}
\citep{hylandAlbarracin2025MindChange}: a Kullback--Leibler asymmetry
that penalises movement away from the current $\C$ and so generates
hysteresis under environmental drift. Together, the two pieces
articulate the joint dynamics by which a community of agents installs,
maintains, and (sometimes failing to) revises its salience priors.

The contribution is technical: we lift a single-agent variational cost
of mind-change \citep{hylandAlbarracin2025MindChange} to a coupled
multi-agent setting on a small-world graph
\citep{wattsStrogatz1998SmallWorld}, integrate it with a precision-as-trust
account of the social channel, and show that the resulting dynamics
admit a clean dissociation in the $\stub \to 0$ limit from
Bayesian rule-induction baselines
\citep{oldenburgZhiXuan2024BayesianRule}.

Section~\ref{sec:related} situates the contribution against immediate

Section~\ref{sec:formal} develops the formal background:

Section~\ref{sec:experiments} outlines the empirical programme.
